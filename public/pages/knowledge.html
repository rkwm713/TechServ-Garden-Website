Spring Gardening Guide

March - May

What to Plant

  • Warm-Season Vegetables
    Tomatoes, peppers, eggplant, squash, cucumbers, beans, corn, okra
  • Herbs
    Basil, cilantro, dill, parsley, oregano, thyme
  • Summer Flowers
    Zinnias, marigolds, sunflowers, cosmos, petunias

Garden Tasks

  • Prepare Garden Beds
    Clean up winter debris, add compost, and prepare soil for spring planting.
  • Set Up Irrigation
    Install or repair irrigation systems before summer heat arrives.
  • Mulch Garden Beds
    Apply 2-3 inches of mulch to conserve moisture and suppress weeds.

Watch Out For

  • Late Frosts
    Be prepared to protect tender plants from unexpected late frosts in March and early April.
  • Early Pests
    Monitor for aphids, cutworms, and other early-season pests as temperatures warm.

Frequently Asked Questions

When is the best time to plant tomatoes in East Texas?

The best time to plant tomatoes in East Texas is after the danger of frost has passed, typically mid-March to early April. Soil temperatures should be at least 60°F for optimal growth. For fall tomatoes, plant in late July to early August for a harvest before the first frost.

How do I deal with the clay soil common in parts of East Texas?

To improve clay soil in East Texas, add plenty of organic matter like compost, aged manure, or leaf mold. Work it into the top 6-8 inches of soil. Adding expanded shale can also help break up clay and improve drainage. Avoid working clay soil when it's too wet or too dry. Building raised beds is another effective solution for gardening in heavy clay areas.

What are the best drought-tolerant plants for East Texas?

Some excellent drought-tolerant plants for East Texas include: Black-eyed Susan, Purple Coneflower, Lantana, Salvia, Rosemary, Sage, Yucca, Texas Sage, Agave, and Ornamental grasses like Muhly grass. For vegetables, okra, cowpeas, peppers, and sweet potatoes are relatively drought-tolerant once established.

How do I protect my garden from East Texas summer heat?

To protect your garden from the intense East Texas summer heat: 1) Apply 2-3 inches of mulch to retain moisture and keep soil cooler, 2) Install shade cloth (30-50%) over sensitive plants during the hottest part of the day, 3) Water deeply in the early morning rather than frequent shallow watering, 4) Use drip irrigation to deliver water directly to plant roots, 5) Plant heat-tolerant varieties appropriate for our region, and 6) Consider creating microclimates by planting taller plants to shade more sensitive ones.
